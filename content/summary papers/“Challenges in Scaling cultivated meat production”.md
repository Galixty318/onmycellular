### Meating the Moment (2025, McNulty et al.)
[DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44319-025-00492-8)

- The first agricultural revolution created the surplus of food necessary for urbanization and increasing occupational diversification from farming. [The second revolution brought about automation, boosting production and incorporating mechanization.] The third revolution introduced high-yielding crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and other enhancements to meet the the demands of the growing human population. 
- A primary hurdle for the industry is the long time-frame which is required to bring products successfully to the market. 
	- The industry has been around for about 10 years--a relatively short time compared to the considerable challenges with adapting high-volume biomedical technologies to high-volume food applications--but this is longer than the typical venture capital timeline. So, following the Gartner cycle, investments would be considered in the trough of disillusionment. 
- The authors are supportive of increased investment from public resources and governments to build infrastructure for the industry. 
- The authors emphasize the importance of developing extensive open-source resources for end-to-end development of cellular agriculture. Transparent communication between researchers and developers is vital for the speed-up of development. 
	- The current **farmer's co-op model**--where farmers pool resources--such as buying supplies in bulk at a discounted rate--can be "adapted to expand cellular agriculture resources," such as for bioreactors systems. 
- The design-build-taste cycle: a version of the pharmaceutical design-build-test cycle.
	- In order to reach the taste cycle, methods for producing large amounts (~40-150 L -> Kgs of 100% cell-based meat) are necessary.
- A **"Turing Test"** (a way to evaluate the ability of a machine to exhibit intelligence via imitating natural dialog) for meat should be developed in order for cultured meat to truly substitute for the conventional stuff. 
- The authors warn against simply sticking to the mindset of "adapting pharmaceutical R&D to cellular agriculture." Those methods were developed with a completely different goal in mind, so simple adaptations are likely insufficient for goals such as high-volume production.
