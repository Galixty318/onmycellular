### Meating the Moment (2025, McNulty et al.)
[DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44319-025-00492-8)

- The first agricultural revolution created the surplus of food necessary for urbanization and increasing occupational diversification from farming. [The second revolution brought about automation, boosting production and incorporating mechanization.] The third revolution introduced high-yielding crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and other enhancements to meet the the demands of the growing human population. 
- A primary hurdle for the industry is the long time-frame which is required to bring products successfully to the market. 
	- The industry has been around for about 10 years--a relatively short time compared to the considerable challenges with adapting high-volume biomedical technologies to high-volume food applications--but this is longer than the typical venture capital timeline. So, following the Gartner cycle, investments would be considered in the trough of disillusionment. 
- The authors are supportive of increased investment from public resources and governments to build infrastructure for the industry. 
- The authors emphasize the importance of developing extensive open-source resources for end-to-end development of cellular agriculture. Transparent communication between researchers and developers is vital for the speed-up of development. 
	- The current **farmer's co-op model**--where farmers pool resources--such as buying supplies in bulk at a discounted rate--can be "adapted to expand cellular agriculture resources," such as for bioreactors systems. 
- The design-build-taste cycle: a version of the pharmaceutical design-build-test cycle.
	- In order to reach the taste cycle, methods for producing large amounts (~40-150 L -> Kgs of 100% cell-based meat) are necessary.
- A **"Turing Test"** (a way to evaluate the ability of a machine to exhibit intelligence via imitating natural dialog) for meat should be developed in order for cultured meat to truly substitute for the conventional stuff. 
- The authors warn against simply sticking to the mindset of "adapting pharmaceutical R&D to cellular agriculture." Those methods were developed with a completely different goal in mind, so simple adaptations are likely insufficient for goals such as high-volume production.

### Trends, challenges, and opportunities for the United States alternative meat and seafood sector: stakeholder-informed perspectives (2026, Wood et al.)
[DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41538-026-00841-4)

- 20 diverse stakeholders in the alternative protein industry were interviewed. But those who were contacted to be interviewed, and had more publicly unfavorable stances against the industry, declined. So, results may be skewed and not wholly representative. 
- **The Boom:** The capital boom into the alt. protein (AP) sector was partly spurred on by the launch of Beyond Meat and Impossible Foods in 2012 and 2016 respectively. Another contributor was Dr. Mark Post's development of the world's first cell-cultivated burger in 2013. The boom developed into a bubble during the pandemic due to people cooking from home more, interest in health was increasing, and supply chain issues appearing. Then, when people returned to their normal lives, demand decreased. The downturn was also attributed to "inflation, not meeting customer taste or price expectations, concerns around APs ultra processed foods (UPFs), and market saturation." 
- For the cellular agriculture, a lack of products on the market is due in part to backlogged regulatory approval processes in the USA. 
- A lot of interest in the cellular agriculture market has also been lost due to a lack of products on the shelves. 
- Environmental, animal welfare, and even nutritive concerns are not enough to convince most uncaptured customers or regulators to accept APs in their regular diet. Price and taste are the most important factors, APs have to be the cheaper and tastier option in order to gain broader acceptance. 
- The private sector alone doesn't have the funds necessary for the AP sector to complete the research and development that needs to happen. Public, governmental, support could look like: "loan guarantees, transferable tax credits, government procurement guarantees or commitments."
- May companies get stuck in the R&D loophole. And a large part of the barrier for getting products out there is the lack of production and R&D infrastructure. Some interviewees emphasized the importance of involving established food distributors as investors in the sector in order to benefit from their existing brand trust, regulatory knowledge, and development and distribution networks. 
- The AP sector needs real innovation, and not just adapt or borrow from existing pharmaceutical technology. 
- Moving from batch to continuous processes is crucial for unit economics. 
- So called "balanced proteins," a mix of conventional meat and AP products, have done well in consumer testing and have sometimes outperformed fully conventional meat products in tastings. Balanced proteins offer a very plausible entrance for AP products into mass market, particularly those of cell-cultivated companies. 
- Investors also believe in the value of producing more products that are ready-to-go, as well as more culinary influencers in the space, so that consumers can get an idea of how these products are best consumed, and so that they get more acclimated to the idea of consuming them. 
- AP seafood is a particularly advantageous space due to the higher base price of seafood and levels of natural contaminants such as heavy metals. The former means there's less optimization that needs to happen to make the price of AP seafood competitive with normal seafood. The latter means the advantages of cell-cultivation--controlled environments and low contamination risk--are even more advantageous in the seafood space. 
- Very few companies--five by the time of this paper's publishing--have regulatory approval from the USDA and FDA. And there seems to be a broad idea that there is a heavy backlog of cell-cultivated products. 
- A majority of the bans on cell-cultivated products seem to be partisan (right). Some participants expressed concern that this restricts consumer choice. 
- A useful point of view to get policy makers' interests would be to emphasize that the US is letting China take the global lead in APs by not investing more resources to streamlining domestic development. Alongside that would be emphasizing domestic food-independance, American job growth, and national security.
- The all vegan or nothing narrative that some companies took earlier on in the industry's existence is not sustainable and likely harmed the perception of APs. Many now see the rebranding from the AP products being replacements and "alternatives" for conventional meat to "complimentary" to them. 
- There is also heavy concern about the shift in consumer consciousness towards associating health with level of processing, so many are turned off by APs due to their processing heavy production. A similar type of fear to that of GMOs could and probably does befall the AP industry considerably.
